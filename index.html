Professional Story

I came to software engineering after years in the Army and EMS, where pressure, accountability, and service shaped the work. That background now shapes how I build software: stay calm, solve the real problem, communicate clearly, and create systems people can trust.

I have now completed my computer science degree and am focused on turning that foundation into a long-term software career. The full reflection below explains the transition in more detail, but the short version is simple: I chose this path because I wanted to build, keep growing, and do meaningful work in a new field.

I caught myself looking in the mirror one day and realized I no longer recognized the person staring back. Two deployments as an Army combat medic had already shaped me, but after coming home I carried that same drive into civilian EMS, moving from EMT to paramedic right as COVID hit. What began as 24-hour shifts quickly turned into endless 48-hour marathons with barely a day to recover.

The crisis pace has since eased, but the damage was done. Most EMTs and paramedics last an average of six years in the field. I pushed through more than fifteen. Somewhere in that grind, the job consumed everything. The reflection staring back was no longer the soldier who carried himself with confidence or the paramedic who wanted to make a difference. It was someone hollowed out by exhaustion. That moment forced me to admit a hard truth: I had built my life entirely around service to others, but in the process, I had lost myself.

So I made a choice. If I no longer recognized the man in the mirror, then it was time to rebuild him. I turned toward computer science not because it was easy, but because it offered something I had not felt in years: the chance to create, solve problems with purpose, and chart a future that was mine to define.

This program challenged me in ways I did not expect. I had to relearn how to learn while juggling a demanding EMS schedule, raising a family of five daughters, and carrying the mental toll of years in uniform and on the ambulance. But the same discipline that carried me through war zones and emergency calls also carried me through debugging code at two in the morning. Programming taught me that success is not about knowing everything. It is about breaking problems down, building solutions step by step, and being willing to fail, learn, and keep going until it works.

Over the course of my studies, I built confidence moving from fundamentals into advanced concepts. Courses in algorithms, data structures, and databases gave me a strong technical base, while projects like DriverPass, Travlr Getaways, and Artemis Financial taught me to apply those skills in realistic scenarios. I came to value not just writing code that runs, but writing code that is secure, modular, and maintainable. My experience in CS-410 with reverse engineering showed me how vulnerable poorly written software can be, and that pushed me toward a security-focused mindset.

The capstone experience pulled everything together. Enhancing and reflecting on my artifacts required me to look at old code with new eyes. I took a reverse-engineered C++ client system and made it more scalable and secure. I revisited a project centered on sorting and searching and demonstrated stronger algorithmic thinking. I rebuilt parts of my MongoDB CRUD dashboard to highlight validation, modular backend design, and safer behavior. Each enhancement was not just a code change. It was proof of growth.

Looking back, the biggest lesson I learned is adaptability. In EMS, no two calls are the same. In software, no two problems are the same. Both fields demand quick thinking, teamwork, and resilience. What I gained from SNHU is the technical foundation and engineering mindset to apply those same qualities in a new career.

Now that I have completed the degree, I am ready to contribute as a software developer and continue growing in backend and security-focused work. My portfolio demonstrates not only the artifacts of my learning, but also the resilience and drive behind them. This career change has given me more than a credential. It has given me a second chance to build a future that challenges me, supports my family, and makes full use of everything I have learned along the way.
